# Clause Lineage

This document exists to reduce novelty friction. PLF is not intended as a license family invented from nothing. It is a modular assembly framework that borrows from familiar open-source and source-available traditions.

## Positioning

PLF should be described as:

- a modular framework built from recognizable licensing patterns
- a policy assembly system for creators who need more nuance than MIT and less rigidity than closed proprietary drafting
- a source-available architecture, not a claim that every clause is unprecedented

## Main Lineage Anchors

### Apache-2.0 style notice, disclaimer, and liability patterns

PLF warranty disclaimer and limitation-of-liability language are intentionally written in the style of familiar permissive-license boilerplate used in Apache-2.0 and related permissive licenses.

Use this talking point:

> The risk-allocation language is not novel in spirit. It follows the same well-understood disclaimer pattern legal teams already see in Apache-style licensing.

### AGPLv3-style network reciprocity

PLF network reciprocity modules are modeled on the core AGPL idea that hosted or remote users should not lose all source-related rights merely because software is delivered as a service instead of as a downloadable package.

Use this talking point:

> The PLF network modules are adaptations of a concept legal teams already know from AGPLv3: SaaS use can trigger disclosure or notice obligations.

### MPL-style modification and file-change notices

PLF file difference, modification disclosure, and modified-fork naming duties are influenced by the same family of concerns seen in file-level copyleft and source-notice licenses such as MPL-style change tracking.

Use this talking point:

> These modules do not invent the idea of modification notices. They formalize familiar “say what changed” obligations in a modular form.

### Creative Commons three-layer presentation

PLF documentation and generator UX deliberately adopt the Creative Commons pattern of:

- legal code
- human-readable deed
- machine-readable metadata

Use this talking point:

> The presentation layer is intentionally familiar: lawyers get legal text, managers get a readable summary, and tooling gets a structured identifier.

## Important Drafting Caution

PLF documentation should say clauses are:

- adapted from
- modeled on
- inspired by
- aligned with the tradition of

Avoid saying a clause is “identical” unless you have performed a literal text comparison and intentionally preserved the wording exactly.
